SoloVoyage: Your Ultimate Solo Travel Safety Guide

Embark on confident and secure solo adventures with these 101 essential tips.

Traveling solo is an extraordinary journey of self-discovery, boundless freedom, and unforgettable experiences. Yet, the independence it offers also necessitates a heightened awareness of personal safety. This comprehensive guide serves as your trusted companion, meticulously detailing 101 actionable tips designed to empower you with the knowledge and vigilance needed to navigate your solo travels with unwavering confidence and ensure your well-being remains paramount. Each tip delves deeper into practical strategies, transforming potential vulnerabilities into opportunities for empowered and secure exploration.

Essential Pre-Trip Safety Foundation

1.

Research Your Destination Like a Detective

Go beyond glossy travel brochures and tourist websites. Check multiple government travel advisory sites (US State Department, UK Foreign Office, Canadian government travel advisories) for current security alerts, health warnings, and political situations. Read local news websites using Google Translate if necessary. Search for recent crime reports in areas you plan to visit. Join destination-specific Facebook groups and Reddit communities where travelers share real experiences. Look up recent reviews on TripAdvisor, but focus on negative reviews to understand potential problems. Research local customs and laws - some countries have strict rules about dress codes, photography, or behavior that could result in arrest. Check if your destination has any ongoing natural disasters, civil unrest, or disease outbreaks. Use Google Street View to virtually explore neighborhoods and get a feel for safety levels.

2.

Master the Art of Sharing Your Plans

Create a comprehensive itinerary document that includes your full name, passport number, emergency contact information, detailed flight information (airline, flight numbers, departure/arrival times), accommodation details (names, addresses, phone numbers, confirmation numbers), planned activities with addresses and times, local transportation plans, and backup contacts in your destination country. Share this with at least two reliable people at home - preferably one family member and one friend. Set up specific check-in times (daily at minimum, but consider twice daily for higher-risk destinations). Create a simple code word system: one word means "I'm safe but can't talk freely," another means "I need help but I'm not in immediate danger," and a third means "call police immediately." Establish what should happen if you miss a check-in (how long to wait before taking action, who to contact first). Consider using apps like Find My Friends or Google Maps location sharing for real-time tracking.

3.

Get the Right Travel Insurance

Standard travel insurance often excludes solo travelers from certain coverages or has inadequate limits. Look for policies that specifically cover solo travel situations. Essential coverage includes: medical evacuation (minimum $1 million coverage - medical flights can cost $100,000-$500,000), comprehensive medical coverage including emergency dental and mental health services, trip cancellation and interruption with "cancel for any reason" options, baggage loss and delay coverage, personal liability insurance, coverage for high-risk activities you might participate in (hiking, scuba diving, etc.), 24/7 emergency assistance hotline with multilingual support, coverage for electronics and valuable items, and political evacuation coverage. Read policy exclusions carefully - many don't cover injuries from alcohol consumption, extreme sports, or pre-existing medical conditions. Consider purchasing from reputable companies like World Nomads, Allianz, or Travel Guard. Annual policies may be more cost-effective for frequent travelers.

4.

Document Everything (The Smart Way)

Take high-quality photos of all important documents using a well-lit, flat surface. Photograph your passport (photo page and any visa pages), driver's license (front and back), credit cards (front only, covering CVV), debit cards, travel insurance policy, accommodation confirmations, flight tickets, vaccination records, prescription medication bottles with dosage information, and any special permits or licenses you'll need. Create a secure folder in cloud storage (Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox) and upload all photos. Email the folder link to yourself and a trusted contact. Create a secure note on your phone with all important numbers (passport, credit cards, insurance policy numbers, etc.). Make two sets of physical copies - keep one in your carry-on bag and another in checked luggage, both separate from originals. Consider laminating physical copies for durability. Write down your blood type, allergies, and emergency medical information on a card to keep in your wallet.

5.

Learn Key Local Phrases That Could Save Your Life

Beyond basic politeness phrases, focus on emergency and safety-related language. Essential phrases include: "I need help" (practice shouting this clearly), "Call the police," "Call an ambulance," "Where is the hospital?," "I don't speak [local language]," "I'm lost," "I'm sick," "Fire!," "Thief!," "Leave me alone," "I don't understand," "How much?" (to avoid being overcharged), "Where is the bathroom?," "I need a doctor," and "Can you help me?" Learn numbers 1-10 and how to ask for directions to important places like police station, hospital, embassy, and airport. Download language apps like Google Translate with offline capabilities, but don't rely solely on technology. Practice pronunciation with native speakers online through language exchange websites. Write phrases phonetically in your notebook as backup. Learn to recognize important written signs like "Exit," "Danger," "Hospital," and "Police."

6.

Choose Safe Neighborhoods to Stay In

Research neighborhoods using multiple sources: government travel advisories often list areas to avoid, local police crime statistics (sometimes available online), travel forums with recent solo traveler experiences, and Google Maps reviews filtered by safety mentions. Look for neighborhoods with good public transportation connections, well-lit streets, regular police presence, nearby hospitals or medical facilities, restaurants and shops that stay open late, and other hotels or hostels nearby. Avoid areas known for high crime rates, red-light districts, political demonstrations, recent terrorist attacks, drug trafficking, or natural disaster risks. Consider staying in business districts or tourist areas for your first visit, even if they're more expensive. Read recent reviews specifically from solo female travelers if applicable. Check if your chosen area has evening curfews or safety warnings. Look for neighborhoods with good walkability scores and avoid places where you'd be dependent on taxis late at night.

7.

Book Your First Night's Accommodation in Advance

Never arrive in a new destination without confirmed accommodation, especially for your first night. Arriving tired, possibly disoriented by time zones, and carrying luggage makes you vulnerable to scams and poor decisions. Book reputable accommodation with: confirmed reservation number, 24-hour front desk service, good online reviews from recent solo travelers, safe location with easy transportation access, and backup contact information. Avoid booking through third-party sites that don't provide direct hotel contact information. Print confirmation emails and save them offline on your phone. Research the route from airport to hotel in advance. Consider booking your first 2-3 nights to give yourself time to adjust and explore other options once you arrive. If staying in hostels, read reviews about security measures, locker availability, and solo traveler experiences. Avoid accommodations that require cash-only payments or have suspicious booking practices.

8.

Plan Your Route from Airport to Hotel

Research all transportation options from your arrival airport to accommodation before you travel. Official airport taxis: locate official taxi stands inside the airport, research typical fare ranges, know if meters are used or if you need to negotiate prices, and get a receipt. Public transportation: download transit apps, purchase tickets or passes online if possible, research operating hours and frequency, identify which stops are safe vs. unsafe, and know backup routes if service is disrupted. Pre-booked transfers: book through reputable companies, get confirmation numbers, know pickup locations, and have contact information for the driver. Ride-sharing apps: confirm which apps operate in your destination, set up accounts before traveling, verify driver and vehicle information, and share trip details with contacts at home. Map out multiple route options and backup plans. Know approximately how long each journey should take to identify if you're being taken on unnecessary detours. Have your hotel address written in local language to show drivers.

9.

Check Your Passport Expiration Date

Many countries require your passport to be valid for 6 months beyond your planned departure date, while others require 3 months. Some countries won't let you board the plane without sufficient validity remaining. Check entry requirements for every country on your itinerary, including layover destinations that might require transit visas. If your passport expires within the required timeframe, renew it immediately - passport renewals can take 6-10 weeks during busy periods, or 2-3 weeks for expedited service with additional fees. Check that your passport has enough blank pages for entry stamps and visas. Consider getting extra pages added if you're a frequent traveler. Keep your old passport if it contains valid visas for future travel. Take photos of your new passport and update all your document copies. Inform your travel insurance company and any pre-booked services about your new passport number.

10.

Register with Your Embassy or Consulate

Most countries offer free registration services for citizens traveling abroad. Register through official government websites: US citizens use STEP (Smart Traveler Enrollment Program), UK citizens use travel advice and consular services, Canadian citizens use Registration of Canadians Abroad service, and Australian citizens use Smartraveller registration. Registration provides: automatic notification of safety and security updates for your destination, easier assistance during emergencies like natural disasters or civil unrest, help with evacuation if necessary, replacement of lost or stolen passports, assistance with medical emergencies, legal assistance if arrested, and contact with family during emergencies. Update your registration if your travel plans change. Know the location and contact information of your embassy or consulate in your destination. Many embassies offer after-hours emergency contact numbers. Some countries have multiple consulates in different cities - know which one is closest to your planned locations.

Essential Packing and Preparation

11.

Use a Money Belt or Hidden Pouch

Invest in a high-quality, comfortable money belt or hidden pouch that you can wear under your clothing all day without discomfort. Look for features like: RFID-blocking material to prevent electronic pickpocketing, moisture-wicking fabric to prevent sweat buildup, multiple compartments for organizing documents and cash, durable zippers and stitching, and comfortable straps that won't chafe. Choose between waist-style money belts (worn around your waist under clothes), neck pouches (hang around your neck under shirts), or leg straps (attach to your thigh). Keep your most important items here: passport, backup credit card, emergency cash (mix of local currency and US dollars), important phone numbers written on paper, and copies of critical documents. Practice putting on and accessing your money belt before traveling so you can do it discreetly. Never access your money belt in public - go to a bathroom or private area. Distribute your money between your money belt, wallet, and other secure locations so you're never carrying everything in one place.

12.

Pack a Comprehensive First Aid Kit

Create a personalized first aid kit based on your destination, planned activities, and medical needs. Essential items include: prescription medications (bring extra in case of delays, keep in original bottles with labels, bring copies of prescriptions), over-the-counter medications (pain relievers like ibuprofen and acetaminophen, anti-diarrheal medication, antihistamine for allergies, motion sickness medication, decongestant, antacid), wound care supplies (adhesive bandages in various sizes, sterile gauze pads, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment), thermometer, tweezers, scissors, instant ice packs, elastic bandages, and any personal medical devices. Research what medications are legal in your destination - some common US medications are banned in other countries. Include a list of your medical conditions, allergies, blood type, and emergency contacts. Consider destination-specific additions like malaria prophylaxis, altitude sickness medication, or water purification tablets. Keep medications in carry-on luggage and bring a doctor's note for prescription drugs.

13.

Download Offline Maps to Your Phone

Don't rely solely on internet connectivity for navigation. Download offline maps before traveling using: Google Maps (download specific areas for offline use, includes basic business information and public transit routes), Maps.me (detailed offline maps with hiking trails and points of interest), City Maps 2Go (offline maps with augmented reality features), or Sygic (includes offline GPS navigation for driving). Download maps for: your destination city and surrounding areas, airports and transportation hubs, areas around your accommodation, neighborhoods you plan to visit, and backup routes between important locations. Test offline maps before traveling to ensure they work without internet. Include screenshots of important routes in your photo gallery as backup. Learn to read physical maps as well - technology can fail. Carry a physical map of your destination city and mark important locations like your hotel, embassy, hospitals, and police stations. Update offline maps regularly as they can become outdated.

14.

Bring Multiple Phone Charging Options

A dead phone can leave you stranded, unable to navigate, call for help, or contact people. Pack: a portable power bank with enough capacity to charge your phone 2-3 times (look for 10,000+ mAh capacity), wall chargers compatible with your destination's outlets, car chargers if you plan to rent a vehicle, charging cables (bring extras in case one breaks), universal travel adapters with multiple plug types, and solar chargers for outdoor adventures. Research your destination's electrical outlets and voltage requirements. Some countries use different voltages that could damage your devices. Consider bringing a power strip with multiple outlets so you can charge several devices at once. Keep charging equipment in your carry-on bag so you can charge devices during travel. Test all charging equipment before leaving home. Consider backup devices like a simple phone that holds charge longer than smartphones, or a satellite communicator for remote areas.

15.

Pack a Whistle or Personal Alarm

Personal safety devices can deter attackers and attract help when needed. Options include: loud whistles (simple, reliable, work without batteries, can be heard from far away), personal alarms (battery-powered devices that emit 120+ decibel sounds when activated), pocket air horns (extremely loud but require careful packing due to pressure), and smartphone alarm apps (convenient but require battery power). Choose devices that are: easy to access quickly, loud enough to be heard over ambient noise, reliable and won't activate accidentally, legal in your destination (some countries restrict certain personal safety devices). Attach safety devices to your keychain or keep them easily accessible in your pocket or bag. Practice using them so you can activate them quickly under stress. Consider carrying multiple types as backup. Learn how to use your phone's emergency SOS features which can automatically call local emergency services and send your location to emergency contacts.

16.

Don't Pack Expensive Jewelry

Valuable jewelry makes you a target for theft and can cause problems at customs or security checkpoints. Leave at home: expensive watches, diamond or precious stone jewelry, gold or platinum items, family heirlooms, engagement rings (consider wearing a simple band instead), and anything that would be devastating to lose. If you must bring jewelry: choose items with sentimental rather than monetary value, consider good-quality costume jewelry instead of real pieces, limit yourself to one or two simple pieces, never wear everything at once, and consider travel insurance that specifically covers jewelry. Research local customs about jewelry - some cultures consider certain items inappropriate or offensive. Be aware that expensive jewelry might indicate wealth and make you a target for scams or theft. Keep valuable items in hotel safes when not wearing them. Take photos of jewelry for insurance purposes before traveling.

17.

Make Multiple Copies of Important Documents

Create a comprehensive document backup system with physical and digital copies. Make copies of: passport (photo page and any visa pages), visas and permits, driver's license, travel insurance policy, flight confirmations, accommodation reservations, credit cards (front only), medical information and prescriptions, emergency contact lists, and important phone numbers. Physical copies: make at least three sets, keep one set in carry-on luggage, one in checked luggage, and give one to a trusted contact at home. Store copies in waterproof plastic bags or containers. Consider laminating copies for durability. Digital copies: scan or photograph documents in high resolution, store in multiple cloud services (Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox), email copies to yourself and a trusted contact, save copies on multiple devices, and password-protect sensitive files. Update copies whenever you get new documents or information changes.

18.

Tell Your Bank You're Traveling

Contact all your financial institutions before traveling to prevent your accounts from being frozen for suspicious activity. Provide: exact travel dates including departure and return, list of countries you'll visit, list of cities where you plan to use cards, phone number where you can be reached abroad, and alternative contact information. Ask about: foreign transaction fees and how to minimize them, daily withdrawal limits for ATMs abroad, which ATM networks they partner with internationally, emergency contact numbers for lost/stolen cards, and procedures for reporting problems while abroad. Consider: opening a bank account with no foreign transaction fees, getting a credit card designed for international travel, setting up account alerts for all transactions, and having backup cards from different banks. Write down customer service numbers that work from abroad (often different from domestic numbers). Know how to call collect or use international calling codes. Set up online banking access and download banking apps before traveling.

19.

Check the Weather Forecast and Research Local Climate

Understanding weather patterns helps you pack appropriately and blend in with locals. Research: current weather conditions and forecasts for your travel dates, seasonal weather patterns and what to expect, average temperatures, rainfall, and humidity levels, extreme weather risks (hurricanes, monsoons, heat waves), appropriate clothing for the local climate and culture, and seasonal considerations (rainy season, high tourist season, etc.). Pack clothing that: helps you blend in with locals rather than standing out as a tourist, is appropriate for local customs and religious sites, can be layered for changing conditions, is quick-drying and easy to wash, and includes rain gear if necessary. Consider the weight and bulk of clothing - pack versatile pieces that can be mixed and matched. Research laundry options at your destination so you can pack lighter. Check if your destination has specific weather-related safety concerns like monsoon flooding or extreme heat warnings.

20.

Pack Light and Smart

Heavy luggage makes you look like a tourist, is harder to manage safely, and slows you down in dangerous situations. Packing strategies: choose versatile clothing that can be mixed and matched, limit yourself to 2-3 pairs of shoes, pack only essential electronics, bring quick-dry fabrics that can be washed and dried overnight, and roll clothes instead of folding to save space. Use packing cubes to organize and compress items. Choose luggage that: has wheels for easy transport, meets airline size and weight restrictions, has sturdy zippers and locks, doesn't look expensive or new, and has external pockets for easy access to important items. Distribute weight evenly and keep heavy items close to your back in backpacks. Pack essential items in your carry-on in case checked luggage is lost. Leave room for souvenirs but be prepared to ship items home if necessary. Test your packed luggage by walking around with it - if it's too heavy or awkward, you're more vulnerable to theft and accidents.

Transportation Safety

21.

Arrive at the Airport Early

Rushing through airports increases stress and the likelihood of making safety mistakes or forgetting important items. Arrive at least: 3 hours early for international flights, 2 hours early for domestic flights, and earlier during busy travel periods or at unfamiliar airports. Use extra time to: double-check you have all important documents, review your travel plans and confirm next steps, charge electronic devices, eat a proper meal to maintain energy, research your destination and transportation options, call home to confirm your departure, and mentally prepare for your journey. Rushing can lead to: forgetting important items in security lines, missing connecting flights and ending up stranded, making poor decisions about transportation, being targeted by scammers who prey on stressed travelers, and exhaustion that affects your judgment upon arrival. Check-in online when possible to avoid long lines. Consider airport lounges for a quiet space to rest and recharge devices.

22.

Keep Valuables in Carry-On Luggage

Never put important or valuable items in checked baggage, which can be lost, stolen, or damaged. Keep in carry-on: passport and travel documents, money and credit cards, prescription medications, electronics (phones, tablets, cameras), important contact information, copies of travel documents, jewelry and valuables, and items you'll need during travel. Pack carry-on bags strategically: keep essential items easily accessible, use TSA-approved locks, organize items in clear bags for easy security screening, and keep liquids in regulation-size containers. Be prepared for carry-on restrictions: know what items are prohibited, have backup plans for restricted items, and consider shipping valuable items separately if necessary. Keep your carry-on bag with you at all times - never leave it unattended, even briefly. Consider carrying important items in a small personal bag that stays with you even if you have to check your carry-on due to size restrictions.

23.

Use Official Taxi Services Only

Avoid unmarked taxis, people who approach offering rides, or vehicles that don't look like legitimate taxis. Identify official taxis by: proper taxi licensing and identification numbers, official taxi stands at airports and transportation hubs, taxi company names and contact information clearly displayed, working meters or official fare schedules, and drivers who can provide identification and receipts. Safety practices: only use taxis from official stands or called through reputable companies, verify the driver's identification matches the taxi registration, share taxi details (license plate, driver name, company) with someone before getting in, sit behind the driver where you can see where you're going, keep doors unlocked until you're sure the driver is legitimate, and have your destination address written down in local language. Be aware of common taxi scams: drivers who claim their meter is broken, taking unnecessarily long routes, claiming you owe extra fees, or working with accomplices to rob passengers. Research typical taxi fares beforehand so you know if you're being overcharged.

24.

Book Airport Transfers in Advance

Pre-arranged transportation is safer than figuring out options when you arrive tired and disoriented. Options include: hotel shuttles (often free or low-cost, ask when booking accommodation), private car services (more expensive but safer and more reliable), shared shuttles (economical but may take longer with multiple stops), and public transportation (research routes and safety considerations). When booking transfers: use reputable companies with good online reviews, get confirmation numbers and contact information, know pickup locations and procedures, confirm driver will have your name/information, and have backup contact numbers in case of problems. Share transfer details with someone at home including: company name and contact information, driver name and vehicle details, expected pickup and arrival times, and your accommodation information. Confirm transfers 24-48 hours before travel. Have backup transportation options researched in case your transfer doesn't show up.

25.

Keep Your Phone Charged During Travel

A charged phone is essential for navigation, communication, emergencies, and accessing important information. Charging strategies: charge devices fully before leaving home, use airport charging stations during layovers, bring portable battery packs for long travel days, pack charging cables in easily accessible locations, and consider airplane charging options if available. Manage battery life by: using airplane mode when signal is poor, closing unnecessary apps, reducing screen brightness, turning off location services for non-essential apps, and using power-saving modes during long flights. Have backup communication plans: know how to use payphones or hotel phones, have important phone numbers written down, know your hotel and embassy contact information, and consider backup devices like a simple phone with better battery life. Download important information for offline access: maps, transportation schedules, hotel confirmations, and emergency contact information.

26.

Don't Tell Strangers You're Traveling Alone

Keeping your solo travel status private reduces your vulnerability to scams, theft, and other dangers. When asked about travel companions: be vague ("I'm meeting friends later" or "my group is arriving tomorrow"), redirect the conversation to other topics, or avoid answering personal questions from strangers. Safe responses to common questions: "Where are you staying?" - give general area, not specific hotel name, "How long are you here?" - be vague about exact dates, "What's your room number?" - never share this information, and "Are you here alone?" - deflect or give non-committal answers. Be particularly cautious with: people who ask too many personal questions, those who offer unsolicited help or seem overly friendly, taxi drivers who probe about your plans, and people who approach you in tourist areas. Trust your instincts if someone makes you uncomfortable with their questions. It's okay to be rude if someone is being inappropriately intrusive.

27.

Keep Belongings With You at All Times

Never leave bags, electronics, or valuables unattended, even for a moment. Common scenarios where people lose vigilance: asking strangers to watch bags while using restrooms, leaving items on seats while getting food, putting bags down while taking photos, checking out of hotels and leaving luggage in lobbies, and falling asleep in public places with items unsecured. Safe practices: take everything with you to restrooms, use bag hooks or hold items while eating, keep cameras and phones attached to your body with straps, use hotel storage services rather than leaving items in lobbies, and stay alert in public transportation and waiting areas. If you must leave items temporarily: use hotel safes or lockers, ask hotel staff to store items securely, or choose trusted establishments with good security. Be aware of distraction techniques used by thieves: people who bump into you, ask for directions, or create commotions while accomplices steal your belongings.

28.

Stay Alert During Layovers

Airports can be confusing and it's easy to get lost, miss connections, or be targeted by criminals. Layover safety tips: confirm your gate and departure time immediately upon arrival, set multiple alarms for boarding time, stay in secure areas past security checkpoints, keep boarding passes and ID easily accessible, and don't drink alcohol if you need to stay alert for connections. Be aware of common airport risks: pickpockets in crowded areas, scammers offering fake services, overcharging for food and services, missing connections due to confusion, and exhaustion leading to poor decisions. Use layover time productively: charge electronic devices, eat proper meals to maintain energy, review your next destination's information, contact home to update your status, and rest if you have long layovers. Avoid: leaving secure areas unless necessary, falling asleep in public areas, trusting strangers who offer help, and consuming too much alcohol or caffeine.

29.

Drink Water and Eat Healthy During Travel

Travel dehydration and poor nutrition affect your judgment, energy, and ability to handle dangerous situations. Hydration: drink water regularly even if you don't feel thirsty, avoid excessive alcohol and caffeine which can dehydrate you, bring empty water bottles to fill after security, and be aware that airplane cabin air is very dry. Nutrition: eat regular meals to maintain stable blood sugar and energy, choose foods that won't upset your stomach, avoid eating too much unfamiliar food at once, and pack healthy snacks for long travel days. Consider the effects of: time zone changes on your eating schedule, altitude changes on your appetite and digestion, stress eating or loss of appetite during travel, and unfamiliar foods that might cause digestive issues. Plan ahead: research food options at airports and during layovers, pack safe snacks that travel well, know dietary restrictions and how to communicate them, and have backup plans for meals if flights are delayed.

30.

Sleep Carefully on Long Flights/Trains

Sleeping during flights requires balancing rest with security concerns. Safe sleeping practices: keep valuables secured in a money belt or bag attached to your body, use a blanket or jacket to cover valuable items, sleep with your bag in front of you where you can feel it, ask flight attendants to wake you for important announcements, and be aware of who's sitting near you. Avoid: putting valuables in seat pockets where they can be forgotten or stolen, falling into deep sleep if you're concerned about fellow passengers, sleeping with expensive items visible, and sleeping through important flight announcements. Prepare for comfortable, secure sleep: bring eye masks and earplugs, use inflatable pillows for support, wear comfortable clothes that allow you to sleep while keeping valuables secure, and choose aisle or window seats rather than middle seats where you're more exposed to other passengers. Consider upgrading to business class for safer, more comfortable sleep on very long flights.

Accommodation Safety

31.

Read Reviews from Other Solo Travelers

Solo travelers face different safety concerns than couples or groups, so seek out reviews from people in similar situations. Look for reviews that mention: safety of the neighborhood, especially at night, security measures at the property, staff helpfulness and trustworthiness, experiences of solo female travelers if applicable, noise levels and ability to sleep safely, and any incidents or concerns about theft or harassment. Filter reviews by: solo travelers, recent stays (within the last 6 months), verified stays, and negative reviews to understand potential problems. Pay attention to recurring themes in reviews about: room security and locks, neighborhood safety, staff behavior, cleanliness and maintenance, and other guests. Use multiple review sites: booking platforms, TripAdvisor, Google Reviews, and travel forums. Be skeptical of properties with only positive reviews or very few reviews. Contact previous guests directly through travel forums if you have specific safety concerns.

32.

Choose Places with 24-Hour Front Desk Service

Having staff available at all times provides security and assistance when you need it most. Benefits of 24-hour front desk: someone to help with emergencies, security presence that deters crime, assistance with late arrivals or early departures, help with local information and directions, and ability to contact authorities if needed. When booking, confirm: actual hours of operation (some claim 24-hour service but actually close late at night), staff language capabilities, emergency procedures and contact information, and security protocols. If 24-hour service isn't available: ask about after-hours contact procedures, confirm you can access your room safely at all hours, understand security measures when staff isn't present, and have backup plans for emergencies. Consider alternatives like: hotels with good security systems, properties in very safe neighborhoods, or accommodations with other guests who can provide assistance if needed.

33.

Look for Good Security Features

Assess security measures before booking and upon arrival. Essential security features: key card access rather than traditional keys, security cameras in common areas (not in rooms), locked building entrances with intercom systems, safes in rooms or at front desk, good lighting in hallways and entrances, and secure parking if you have a rental car. Red flags: easy access to rooms from outside, broken locks or security systems, poor lighting in corridors or entrances, no identification required for guests, and staff who seem untrustworthy or unprofessional. Upon arrival: test all locks on doors and windows, locate emergency exits and fire extinguishers, identify escape routes from your room, note security camera locations, and introduce yourself to front desk staff. If security seems inadequate: request a room change to a more secure location, ask about additional security measures, or consider changing accommodations entirely. Don't hesitate to leave if you feel unsafe.

34.

Avoid Ground Floor Rooms

Ground floor rooms are easier targets for break-ins and provide easier escape routes for criminals. Request rooms on: second or third floors (high enough to deter break-ins but low enough for emergency evacuation), floors with good security access, and areas with good lighting and visibility. Avoid: ground floor rooms with windows or doors facing parking lots or alleys, rooms near emergency exits that might not be properly secured, basement or semi-basement rooms, and rooms in isolated areas of the building. If you must stay on the ground floor: ensure windows have secure locks and consider additional security measures, keep curtains closed so people can't see inside, be extra vigilant about locking doors and windows, and consider requesting a room change if you feel unsafe. In hostels: choose upper bunks if possible, use lockers for all valuables, and be aware of who has access to your sleeping area.

35.

Test Locks on Windows and Doors

Always check security measures immediately upon arrival in your room. Door security: test the main door lock, deadbolt, and chain lock, ensure the door closes and locks properly, check that the peephole provides a clear view, and verify that the door frame is sturdy. Window security: test all window locks including those on balconies, check that windows can't be opened from outside, ensure curtains provide privacy, and note if windows are accessible from fire escapes or balconies. If locks don't work properly: request a room change immediately, ask maintenance to fix problems before accepting the room, and document issues with photos in case of problems later. Additional security measures: use portable door alarms or doorstop alarms, block sliding doors with wooden dowels or security bars, and consider bringing additional locks for extra security. Never stay in a room with broken or inadequate locks.

36.

Use Door Chains and Security Latches

Even in hotels with key card access, use all available security measures. How to use security features: engage deadbolts and chain locks every time you enter your room, use door latches when you're in the room, and check that security devices work properly. When someone knocks: use the peephole to identify visitors, don't open the door for strangers, verify the identity of anyone claiming to be hotel staff by calling the front desk, and use the door chain to crack the door open slightly while keeping the chain engaged. If your room doesn't have adequate security devices: request a room with better security features, consider portable security devices like door alarms or doorstop wedges, and ask hotel staff about additional security measures. Be aware that some criminals pose as hotel staff to gain access to rooms - always verify identity through the front desk before opening your door.

37.

Keep Your Room Number Private

Your room number is sensitive information that should only be shared with hotel staff and trusted contacts. Don't share room numbers with: other guests, people you meet at the hotel, taxi drivers or delivery people, or anyone who asks casually. When hotel staff mention your room number: ask them to write it down instead of saying it aloud, request they speak quietly, and be aware of who might overhear. If someone claims to need your room number: ask them to contact you through the front desk, verify their identity independently, and be suspicious of anyone who insists on having your room number. Safe practices: memorize your room number rather than writing it on key cards, don't discuss your room location in public areas, and ask hotel staff to be discreet when providing assistance. If you suspect someone inappropriate has your room number: request a room change immediately and inform hotel management.

38.

Use Hotel Safes for Valuables

Hotel safes provide secure storage for items you don't need to carry with you. What to store in safes: passport (keep a copy with you), extra cash and credit cards, expensive jewelry, electronics you're not using, backup keys, and important documents. Safe usage tips: choose a personal code that's not obvious (avoid birthdays or common numbers), test the safe before storing items, keep the safe locked even when empty, and never share your code with anyone. If your room doesn't have a safe: ask if the hotel offers safe deposit boxes at the front desk, consider bringing a portable travel safe, or keep valuables with you at all times. Document what you put in the safe: take photos of items, keep receipts for expensive items, and maintain a list of what's stored. If the safe malfunctions: contact hotel staff immediately and don't leave items in a broken safe.

39.

Know Where Emergency Exits Are

Familiarize yourself with emergency procedures and exit routes as soon as you arrive. Locate: fire exits closest to your room, alternative exit routes in case primary routes are blocked, fire extinguishers and emergency equipment, assembly points outside the building, and emergency contact information. Study the fire escape plan posted on your door and walk the routes to familiarize yourself with them. Count the number of doors between your room and the nearest exit so you can find it in darkness or smoke. Note: whether exits are clearly marked and well-lit, if exit doors are locked or require key cards, and any obstacles that might impede evacuation. In high-rise buildings: understand evacuation procedures, locate stairwells (never use elevators during emergencies), and know if you should evacuate or shelter in place depending on the emergency. Practice emergency procedures mentally and have a plan for grabbing essential items quickly if you need to evacuate.

40.

Don't Open Your Door for Strangers

Never open your hotel room door for anyone you don't know or weren't expecting. Common scenarios to be cautious about: people claiming to be hotel maintenance, housekeeping staff outside of normal hours, delivery people you didn't order from, other guests asking for help, and anyone claiming there's an emergency. Verification procedures: use the peephole to see who's at your door, ask for identification and employee numbers, call the front desk to verify the person's identity and reason for being there, and don't open the door until you're completely sure. If someone claims to be hotel staff: ask them to wait while you call the front desk, request they return at a more convenient time, and remember that legitimate staff will understand your caution. Red flags: people who seem rushed or pushy, those who can't provide proper identification, anyone who discourages you from calling the front desk, and people who claim urgent situations that require immediate entry. If you feel threatened: don't open the door, call hotel security or front desk immediately, make noise to alert other guests, and call local emergency services if necessary. Trust your instincts - it's better to be rude than unsafe.

Daily Safety While Exploring

41.

Stay in Well-Lit, Busy Areas

Criminals prefer isolated areas where they won't be seen or interrupted. Choose routes and locations that are: well-lit with functioning street lights, busy with pedestrian traffic, visible to shopkeepers and residents, patrolled by police or security, and have escape routes if needed. Avoid: dark alleys and poorly lit streets, empty parks or isolated areas, construction zones with limited visibility, areas that locals warn you to avoid, and places that feel deserted even during daytime. Plan your routes: research safe walking routes between destinations, identify well-lit areas for evening activities, know where police stations and safe havens are located, and have backup routes if your primary path feels unsafe. Time your activities: avoid being out alone very late at night, plan to return to your accommodation before areas become deserted, and adjust your schedule based on local safety patterns. If you find yourself in an unsafe area: move quickly to a busy, well-lit location, enter a shop or restaurant, call a taxi rather than walking, and don't be afraid to ask for help.

42.

Walk with Confidence

Confident body language makes you less likely to be targeted by criminals who look for vulnerable victims. Project confidence by: standing tall with shoulders back, making brief eye contact with people around you, walking at a steady, purposeful pace, keeping your head up and looking aware of your surroundings, and avoiding behavior that signals uncertainty like constantly checking maps or looking lost. Dress appropriately: choose clothing that helps you blend in with locals, avoid items that make you stand out as a tourist, wear comfortable shoes you can walk quickly in, and keep expensive items hidden. If you're lost: step into a shop or restaurant to check directions privately, ask for help from shopkeepers or police rather than strangers on the street, and avoid looking confused or vulnerable in public. Practice confident walking: rehearse walking purposefully even when you're unsure, learn to check your phone or map discreetly, and develop the habit of appearing alert and aware.

43.

Keep Your Phone Visible but Secure

Your phone is essential for safety but also valuable to thieves. Safe phone practices: keep your phone easily accessible for emergencies, use a phone case with a wrist strap or lanyard, avoid using your phone in crowded areas where it might be grabbed, and be aware of your surroundings when using your phone on the street. Security measures: set up phone locks with passcodes or biometric security, enable remote wipe capabilities in case of theft, use "Find My Phone" features, and avoid storing sensitive information in easily accessible apps. When using your phone in public: position yourself against a wall or in a corner so no one can approach from behind, keep conversations brief and private, avoid displaying expensive phones unnecessarily, and put your phone away quickly after use. If your phone is stolen: report it to local police immediately, contact your phone company to suspend service, use remote wipe features to protect your data, and have backup communication methods available.

44.

Trust Your Gut Feelings

Your intuition often recognizes danger before your conscious mind does. Pay attention to feelings of: unease or discomfort in certain areas, suspicion about people's motives, concern about situations that seem "off," and anxiety about places or people. Don't ignore these feelings because: you're worried about being rude, you think you're overreacting, others seem comfortable with the situation, or you don't want to change your plans. When you feel unsafe: leave the area immediately, seek help from authorities or trusted people, call someone to let them know your situation, and don't worry about seeming paranoid. Common situations that trigger gut feelings: people following you, areas that suddenly become deserted, individuals who pay too much attention to you, situations where you feel trapped or unable to leave, and people whose behavior seems inconsistent or suspicious. Develop your intuition: practice paying attention to your feelings, reflect on past experiences when your instincts were correct, and learn to distinguish between normal anxiety and genuine warning signals.

45.

Don't Wear Headphones in Unfamiliar Places

Headphones block important audio cues about your environment and make you an easy target. Stay alert by: keeping your ears open to hear approaching vehicles, people, or danger, being aware of conversations around you that might indicate problems, listening for emergency sirens or alarms, and maintaining awareness of your surroundings. If you must use headphones: keep volume low enough to hear ambient sounds, use only one earbud so one ear remains open, choose open-back headphones that don't completely block sound, and remove them immediately if you feel unsafe. Be especially cautious with headphones: in crowded areas where pickpockets operate, when walking alone at night, in unfamiliar neighborhoods, while using public transportation, and in areas with vehicle traffic. Alternative options: use bone conduction headphones that don't block your ears, listen to audio at low volumes, or save music and podcasts for safe environments like your hotel room.

46.

Research Public Transportation Routes

Understanding local transportation systems before you need them prevents you from getting lost or stranded in unsafe areas. Research: route maps and schedules, safe vs. unsafe stops and areas, payment methods and where to buy tickets, operating hours and frequency, and emergency procedures. Safety considerations: which routes are well-patrolled and safe, areas to avoid especially at night, whether transportation is reliable, and alternative routes if services are disrupted. Practical preparation: download transportation apps, buy passes or tickets in advance when possible, learn key phrases for asking directions, and identify landmarks near important stops. Know: how to recognize official transportation vs. unlicensed operators, typical costs to avoid being overcharged, emergency contact numbers for transportation authorities, and backup transportation options if needed. Practice using the system: take a practice trip during daylight hours, familiarize yourself with stations and stops, and understand how to transfer between different lines or modes of transport.

47.

Keep Your Bag in Front on Crowded Transport

Pickpockets often work in crowded transportation where they can grab items and disappear quickly. Protective strategies: wear backpacks on your front in crowded areas, keep handbags zipped and held securely against your body, use bags with multiple compartments to separate valuables, and keep important items in inside pockets or money belts. Be aware of common pickpocket techniques: people who bump into you or create distractions, individuals who crowd you unnecessarily, groups working together to surround victims, and thieves who grab bags through closing doors. Stay vigilant: watch for people paying unusual attention to your belongings, be aware of hands reaching toward your bag or pockets, notice if someone follows you or positions themselves strategically, and trust your instincts if someone makes you uncomfortable. If you suspect pickpocket activity: move away from suspicious individuals, check your belongings discretely, alert authorities if possible, and avoid confronting suspected thieves directly.

48.

Sit Near the Driver or Conductor

Transportation staff can provide assistance and their presence deters criminal activity. Choose seats: near the driver or conductor where staff can see you, in well-lit areas of the vehicle, with good visibility of exits, and away from isolated sections. Benefits of sitting near staff: immediate access to help if problems arise, deterrent effect on potential criminals, better information about stops and routes, and assistance with language barriers if needed. Avoid: empty sections of buses or trains, seats that face away from the driver, areas near doors where thieves might grab bags and escape, and corners where you might be trapped. If staff isn't present: sit near other passengers who seem trustworthy, choose seats with good visibility of your surroundings, stay alert to what's happening around you, and be prepared to move if the situation changes. Communication with staff: learn basic phrases to ask for help, know how to signal distress, and understand emergency procedures for the type of transportation you're using.

49.

Have Transportation Tickets Ready

Fumbling for tickets or money makes you vulnerable and identifies you as a tourist. Preparation strategies: buy tickets in advance when possible, keep exact change readily available, learn payment procedures before boarding, and understand ticket validation requirements. Organize your money: keep transportation money separate from other cash, use small bills to avoid displaying large amounts, have backup payment methods available, and know typical costs to avoid being overcharged. Safety while paying: don't pull out large amounts of cash in public, be aware of who's watching when you handle money, complete transactions quickly and efficiently, and put change away immediately. Digital payments: set up mobile payment apps if available, understand how contactless payments work, have backup methods if technology fails, and protect your payment information from theft.

50.

Pay Attention to Your Stop

Missing your stop can leave you in an unfamiliar or unsafe area, especially at night. Stay alert by: following your route on a map app, listening for stop announcements, watching for familiar landmarks, setting phone alarms for your approximate arrival time, and asking other passengers for help if needed. Preparation: know the names of stops before and after your destination, understand what your stop looks like, learn key phrases to ask about stops, and have backup plans if you miss your stop. If you miss your stop: stay calm and assess your options, find a safe way to return to your intended destination, avoid getting off in isolated or unsafe areas, and call for help if you feel unsafe. Technology helps: use GPS tracking to monitor your location, download transportation apps with real-time updates, and enable location sharing with trusted contacts so they know where you are.

Money and Valuables Protection

51.

Use ATMs Inside Banks or Hotels

Indoor ATMs are safer than standalone street machines that are easier for criminals to tamper with or monitor. Preferred ATM locations: inside bank branches during business hours, in hotel lobbies with security presence, in shopping malls with good lighting and foot traffic, and in airports or other secure facilities. ATM safety practices: check for suspicious devices attached to card readers or keypads, cover your hand when entering PIN numbers, be aware of people watching you or standing too close, and complete transactions quickly. Avoid: ATMs in isolated locations, machines that look damaged or have unusual attachments, locations where you feel unsafe or exposed, and ATMs surrounded by people who might be working together. If an ATM seems suspicious: don't use it and find another location, report suspicious devices to authorities, and consider using a different ATM even if it's less convenient. After using ATMs: put cash away immediately, check your account balance to ensure the transaction was correct, and monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity.

52.

Don't Count Money in Public

Handling cash in public attracts unwanted attention and identifies you as carrying money. Discrete money management: count money in private locations like bathrooms or your hotel room, organize bills before going out so you know what you have, keep small bills easily accessible for purchases, and avoid displaying large amounts of cash. When making purchases: have approximate amounts ready, complete transactions quickly, put change away immediately, and don't count change in front of others. If you must handle money in public: do it discretely and quickly, be aware of who's watching, use your body to shield money from view, and move to a safer location if possible. Plan your cash needs: estimate how much you'll need for activities, break large bills into smaller denominations in private, keep emergency cash hidden separately, and avoid carrying more cash than necessary.

53.

Carry Small Bills

Having small denominations prevents you from flashing large bills and makes transactions smoother. Benefits: easier to make exact payments, reduces need for change, prevents vendors from claiming they can't make change, and less attractive to thieves than large bills. How to get small bills: exchange money at banks which often provide better denominations, ask hotels to break large bills, make purchases at larger stores that can provide change, and plan ahead by requesting small bills when exchanging money. Use small bills for: tips and gratuities, street vendors and small shops, public transportation, and situations where you want to avoid displaying wealth. Keep larger bills: hidden in your money belt, separate from your daily spending money, for major purchases or emergencies, and in secure locations in your accommodation.

54.

Have a Backup Card Hidden Separately

If your primary cards are stolen, a backup card keeps you from being stranded without money. Backup strategies: keep one credit card and one debit card from different banks, store backup cards in your money belt or hidden location, use cards from different payment networks (Visa and Mastercard), and inform someone at home about your backup card details. Card separation: never keep all cards in the same wallet or location, store backup cards in different bags or clothing, consider leaving one card locked in your hotel safe, and avoid using backup cards unless necessary. If your primary cards are compromised: contact banks immediately to report theft, activate backup cards if they've been suspended, use backup cards for essential expenses only, and arrange for replacement cards to be sent if you're traveling long-term. Prepare backup cards: notify banks about travel plans for all cards, test backup cards before traveling, know contact numbers for all card companies, and understand procedures for reporting lost or stolen cards from abroad.

55.

Divide Your Money Between Multiple Places

Never carry all your money in one location - if it's stolen or lost, you'll be left with nothing. Money distribution strategies: keep daily spending money in your wallet, store backup cash in a money belt, hide emergency money in different locations, and keep some money locked in your accommodation safe. Recommended distribution: 25% in your regular wallet for daily expenses, 50% in a money belt or hidden pouch, 15% in a backup location like a hidden pocket, and 10% as emergency cash in your luggage or accommodation. Consider different hiding places: inside shoes or socks, sewn into clothing, in toiletry containers, in book pages, or in electronic device cases. Currency diversification: carry both local currency and US dollars (widely accepted for emergencies), have different denominations available, and consider traveler's checks for backup (though less common now). If you're robbed: give up the money in your wallet but protect your hidden reserves, don't reveal other money locations, and use backup funds to get to safety.

56.

Use Crossbody Bags with Zippers

The right bag design makes theft much more difficult. Secure bag features: crossbody straps that can't be easily cut, zippers rather than open tops, multiple compartments for organization, RFID-blocking material for card protection, and comfortable straps for all-day wear. Avoid: bags that hang loosely from one shoulder, purses with magnetic closures, bags with outside pockets containing valuables, and expensive-looking bags that attract attention. Wearing techniques: keep the bag in front of your body in crowded areas, hold the bag against your body with your arm, keep zippers closed and facing your body, and stay aware of people reaching toward your bag. Additional security: use small locks on zippers, attach important items to the bag with clips or cords, and consider cut-resistant bag straps in high-crime areas. If someone tries to grab your bag: let it go rather than risk injury, carry only replaceable items in easily accessed areas, and keep your most important items in harder-to-reach places.

57.

Keep Bags Closed and in Front in Crowds

Crowded areas provide cover for pickpockets and bag-snatchers. Crowd safety techniques: wear backpacks on your front in crowded areas, keep handbags zipped and held tightly, be extra vigilant in tourist areas and public transportation, and watch for people who get unnecessarily close. Recognize pickpocket environments: busy markets and shopping areas, crowded public transportation, tourist attractions with dense crowds, festivals and events, and anywhere people are distracted. Stay alert for: people who bump into you repeatedly, individuals who crowd you unnecessarily, groups working together to surround victims, and thieves who grab bags through closing doors. Stay vigilant: watch for people paying unusual attention to your belongings, be aware of hands reaching toward your bag or pockets, notice if someone follows you or positions themselves strategically, and trust your instincts if someone makes you uncomfortable. If you suspect pickpocket activity: move away from suspicious individuals, check your belongings discretely, alert authorities if possible, and avoid confronting suspected thieves directly.

58.

Don't Put Valuables in Back Pockets

Back pockets are the easiest targets for pickpockets and are more likely to lose items when sitting. Use front pockets for: small amounts of daily spending money, items you need to access frequently, and things that won't create bulges that advertise their presence. Back pockets should never contain: wallets with important cards or large amounts of money, phones or electronics, passports or travel documents, or anything you can't afford to lose. Better storage options: front pockets with buttons or zippers, inside jacket pockets, money belts worn under clothing, and secure bag compartments. Be aware of pickpocket techniques: people who bump into you from behind, individuals who create distractions while accomplices steal, and thieves who work in crowded areas where contact seems normal. If you must use back pockets: only carry items you can afford to lose, check pockets frequently, and be extra aware in crowded situations.

59.

Use Travel Locks on Luggage

While locks won't stop determined thieves, they deter casual theft and tampering. Lock types: TSA-approved locks that security can open without cutting, cable locks for securing bags to fixed objects, and combination locks that don't require keys. What to lock: main compartments of checked luggage, zippers on carry-on bags, and bags when leaving them in accommodations. Locking strategies: use locks to slow down thieves rather than stop them completely, combine locks with other security measures, and understand that locks primarily deter opportunistic theft. Limitations: determined thieves can cut most travel locks, airport security may cut locks if needed, and locks can break during rough handling. Best practices: take photos of locked luggage before checking, use locks with bright colors that make tampering obvious, and inspect locks for damage after flights. Additional security: wrap luggage in plastic at airports, use hard-shell suitcases that are harder to break into, and keep valuables in carry-on luggage regardless of locks.

60.

Take Photos of Your Belongings

Documentation helps with insurance claims and police reports if items are stolen. What to photograph: expensive electronics with serial numbers visible, jewelry and valuable items, luggage and bags, important documents, and unique or sentimental items. Photography tips: take clear, well-lit photos, include serial numbers and identifying marks, photograph items from multiple angles, and store photos in cloud storage for access anywhere. Use photos for: insurance claims if items are stolen, police reports that require detailed descriptions, customs declarations for valuable items, and personal records of what you're traveling with. Update photos: before each trip, when you acquire new items, and if you make significant purchases while traveling. Additional documentation: keep receipts for expensive items, maintain lists of serial numbers and model numbers, and consider creating video inventories of all your belongings. If items are stolen: provide photos to police immediately, submit detailed claims to insurance companies, and use photos to help identify recovered items.

Download Safety Apps Before Travel

Technology can provide crucial assistance in emergency situations. Essential safety apps: local emergency services apps, ride-sharing apps like Uber or Lyft, translation apps for communication, offline maps for navigation, and personal safety apps that can alert contacts. Personal safety app features: panic buttons that alert emergency contacts, GPS tracking that shares your location, automatic check-in reminders, loud alarms to attract attention, and integration with local emergency services. Research destination-specific apps: local taxi or transportation apps, weather and emergency alert apps, apps for reporting crimes or safety concerns, and official tourism apps with safety information. Keep apps updated and ensure your phone is compatible with local networks.

62.

Use a VPN on Public Wi-Fi

VPN benefits: encrypts your internet connection, hides your browsing activity from potential hackers, allows access to geo-restricted content, and protects sensitive information like passwords and financial data. Using VPNs safely: connect to the VPN before accessing public Wi-Fi, avoid accessing sensitive accounts even with VPN protection, log out of important accounts when finished, and turn off automatic Wi-Fi connections to prevent joining unsecured networks. Location sharing risks: burglars knowing you're away from home, strangers tracking your movements, criminals targeting tourists in specific areas, and revealing patterns in your travel schedule. Privacy settings: make accounts private so only friends can see posts, review who can see your location information, turn off automatic location tagging, and regularly audit your privacy settings. What not to share: real-time check-ins at hotels or tourist attractions, photos of boarding passes or travel documents, exact travel dates and itineraries, and information about being alone or vulnerable. If you want to share travel experiences: post with delays, avoid identifying specific locations, don't mention being alone, and consider saving posts as drafts to publish later.

64.

Keep Your Phone Charged

Charging strategies: carry portable battery packs with sufficient capacity, bring charging cables compatible with local outlets, use power-saving modes to extend battery life, and take advantage of charging opportunities throughout the day. Battery management: close unnecessary apps that drain battery, reduce screen brightness and timeout settings, turn off location services for non-essential apps, and use airplane mode in areas with poor signal to prevent battery drain. Backup power sources: solar chargers for outdoor activities, hand-crank emergency chargers, car chargers if you're driving, and extra battery packs for extended trips. Find charging opportunities: airports and transportation hubs, restaurants and cafes, hotels and accommodations, and public libraries or internet cafes. Emergency considerations: keep one device charged for emergencies only, know how to make emergency calls with low battery, and have important phone numbers written down in case your phone dies.

65.

Save Important Phone Numbers

Don't rely solely on internet access or cloud storage for critical contact information. Essential numbers to save: local emergency services (police, fire, medical), your embassy or consulate, accommodation contact information, transportation companies (taxi, airlines), your bank and credit card companies, and emergency contacts at home. Save numbers in multiple formats: in your phone's contacts, written on paper kept separate from your phone, stored in cloud services, and memorized for the most critical numbers. Include: international dialing codes, after-hours emergency numbers, collect call procedures, and alternative contact methods. Research local emergency numbers: many countries use 112 as a universal emergency number, but some have specific numbers for police, fire, and medical services. Keep numbers accessible: don't password-protect emergency contact lists, make sure numbers work from international locations, and test important numbers before you need them.

66.

Don't Post That You're Traveling Alone

Revealing that you're traveling solo makes you a target for criminals and can compromise your safety. Information to keep private: specific details about traveling alone, exact travel dates and locations, hotel names and addresses, daily itineraries and plans, and vulnerabilities or concerns you have. Safe posting alternatives: mention travel plans in general terms, avoid real-time location sharing, post photos and updates after you've left locations, and save detailed travel stories for after you return home. Social media risks: strangers who follow your movements, criminals who target solo travelers, people who know you're away from home, and inadvertent revelation of personal information. Privacy protection: make your profile private, limit personal information in your bio, don't share travel plans publicly, and be selective about who you accept as friends.

67.

Turn Off Location Services for Social Apps

Location service risks: apps sharing your real-time location, data being sold to third parties, location history being stored indefinitely, and inadvertent revelation of sensitive locations. Apps to check: social media platforms, photo apps that geo-tag images, fitness apps that track routes, dating apps that show proximity, and weather apps that use location. Privacy settings: turn off location sharing for non-essential apps, review which apps have location access, disable location history storage, and regularly audit app permissions. Exceptions for essential safety: keep location services on for maps and navigation apps, emergency services apps, and trusted friend/family tracking apps. Regular maintenance: review app permissions monthly, delete apps you don't use, update privacy settings when apps update, and understand what data apps collect about you.

68.

Be Careful About New Friend Requests

Red flags in friend requests: profiles with very few friends or posts, accounts created recently, people who claim to know you but you don't remember, profiles with stolen or stock photos, and accounts that immediately ask personal questions after connecting. Safe practices: don't accept requests from strangers while traveling, verify the identity of people who claim to know you, wait until you return home to review friend requests, and be cautious about sharing personal information with new online connections. Information criminals seek: travel plans and itineraries, hotel and accommodation information, whether you're traveling alone, valuable items you're carrying, and your daily routines and schedule. Protect yourself: make your profile private, limit personal information in your bio, don't share travel plans publicly, and be selective about who you accept as friends.

69.

Don't Share Travel Plans Online

Detailed travel information gives criminals a roadmap to target you. Information to keep private: specific hotels and accommodations, exact travel dates and flight information, daily itineraries and planned activities, transportation details, and vulnerable times when you'll be alone or distracted. Why criminals want this information: to time thefts when you're away from your accommodation, to target you in specific locations, to plan scams based on your interests, and to identify when you're most vulnerable. Safe sharing alternatives: speak generally about regions rather than specific cities, share experiences after they happen, avoid posting real-time updates, and save detailed travel stories for personal conversations. Audience considerations: even trusted friends might inadvertently share your information, employer social media policies might expose your travel plans, and family members might not understand security implications of sharing details.

70.

Use Private Messaging Instead of Public Posts

Keep travel communications private to control who has access to your information. Private communication benefits: control over who sees your information, ability to share more details safely, reduced risk of strangers accessing your plans, and better security for sensitive information. What to share privately: specific travel concerns or safety issues, detailed itineraries with trusted contacts, real-time location updates with family, and personal experiences that might reveal vulnerabilities. Public posting risks: strangers gaining access to personal information, criminals using posts to target you, inadvertent revelation of security information, and permanence of public posts that can be referenced later. Food and Drink Safety

71.

Eat at Busy Restaurants

High customer turnover usually indicates fresh food and reduces your risk of foodborne illness. Signs of good restaurants: consistently busy with locals (not just tourists), high turnover of food indicating freshness, clean appearance and good hygiene practices, proper food storage and preparation visible, and positive reviews from recent customers. Red flags: empty restaurants in busy areas, food that's been sitting out for long periods, poor hygiene or cleanliness, flies or pests around food, and establishments that seem to cater only to tourists. Research restaurants: read recent reviews focusing on food safety, ask locals for recommendations, observe restaurants before eating there, and trust your instincts about cleanliness. Food safety indicators: hot food served hot, cold food kept properly chilled, fresh ingredients visible, clean utensils and dishes, and staff following proper hygiene practices.

72.

Be Cautious with Street Food

Street food can be delicious and safe, but requires careful selection to avoid illness. Safe street food practices: choose vendors with high turnover and long lines of locals, watch food being prepared fresh to order, ensure food is cooked thoroughly and served hot, avoid raw or undercooked items, and select vendors with clean preparation areas. Risky street food: items that have been sitting out for long periods, raw vegetables or fruits you can't peel yourself, ice cream or dairy products without proper refrigeration, water or drinks from questionable sources, and food from vendors with poor hygiene practices. Gradual introduction: start with small amounts to test your stomach's reaction, stick to cooked foods for your first few days, avoid street food if you're already feeling unwell, and have medication available for stomach problems. Trust your senses: if food smells off or looks questionable, don't eat it, observe the vendor's hygiene practices, and choose vendors that look clean and organized.

73.

Drink Bottled Water When Necessary

In areas with questionable water quality, bottled water prevents serious illness. When to drink bottled water: in developing countries with poor water infrastructure, areas affected by natural disasters, places where locals advise against tap water, and any time you're unsure about water safety. Bottled water safety: check that seals are intact and haven't been refilled, choose reputable brands, avoid ice made from tap water, and use bottled water for brushing teeth if tap water isn't safe. Alternatives to bottled water: water purification tablets or drops, portable water filters, UV sterilization devices, and boiling water for at least one minute. Hidden water sources: ice in drinks, fruits and vegetables washed in tap water, soup broths that may use tap water, and beverages that might be diluted with tap water. If you get sick from water: stay hydrated with safe fluids, seek medical attention if symptoms are severe, avoid dairy and alcohol until recovered, and consider oral rehydration solutions.

74.

Watch How Food is Prepared

Observing food preparation helps you identify potential health risks. What to look for: clean cooking surfaces and utensils, proper food storage and refrigeration, staff washing hands frequently, separation of raw and cooked foods, and appropriate cooking temperatures. Red flags: reusing utensils between raw and cooked foods, staff not washing hands, food stored at room temperature, flies or pests around preparation areas, and shortcuts in cooking that might not kill bacteria. Kitchen cleanliness: clean uniforms and aprons on staff, organized and tidy preparation areas, proper waste disposal, evidence of regular cleaning, and compliance with local health standards. Food handling: fresh ingredients being used, proper thawing of frozen foods, immediate service of hot foods, and safe storage of leftovers. If a kitchen looks unsafe: choose a different restaurant, don't be afraid to leave before ordering, trust your instincts about cleanliness, and report serious health violations to local authorities.

75.

Eat Fruits You Can Peel Yourself

Fruits with removable peels reduce your risk of consuming contaminated water or bacteria. Safe fruit choices: bananas, oranges, mangoes, avocados, and other fruits where you remove the peel yourself. Risky fruits: pre-cut fruit salads, berries that can't be peeled, fruits washed in questionable water, and items that have been sitting out for long periods. Preparation safety: wash your hands before peeling fruit, use clean utensils if available, peel fruit yourself rather than having others do it, and eat freshly peeled fruit immediately. Fruit safety tips: choose fruits that look fresh and unblemished, avoid fruit that's been pre-prepared, buy from reputable vendors, and store fruit properly if you're not eating it immediately. If you're unsure about fruit safety: stick to packaged items from reputable stores, avoid fruit from street vendors in high-risk areas, and consider bringing fruit from home for the first few days of travel.

76.

Limit Alcohol Consumption

Excessive drinking impairs your judgment and makes you vulnerable to crime and accidents. Alcohol safety guidelines: drink less than you would at home, never drink alone in unfamiliar places, alternate alcoholic drinks with water, and eat food before and while drinking. Risks of overdrinking: poor decision-making about safety, increased vulnerability to theft and assault, dangerous situations you might not recognize when sober, and inability to respond appropriately to emergencies. Safe drinking practices: stick to reputable establishments, watch bartenders prepare your drinks, never accept drinks from strangers, and avoid drinking games or excessive consumption encouraged by others. Know your limits: understand how altitude, heat, and travel fatigue affect alcohol tolerance, be aware that different countries have different alcohol strengths, and recognize signs that you've had too much. Alternative activities: explore local culture through food and non-alcoholic experiences, enjoy daytime activities that don't involve drinking, and socialize in environments that don't center around alcohol.

77.

Never Accept Drinks from Strangers

Accepting drinks from strangers puts you at risk of drink spiking and other crimes. Why people spike drinks: to rob victims while they're incapacitated, to facilitate sexual assault, to kidnap or traffic victims, and to make victims vulnerable to other crimes. Signs of drink spiking: feeling much more intoxicated than expected, memory gaps or confusion, nausea or vomiting beyond normal alcohol effects, difficulty speaking or moving, and feeling like you're losing control. Prevention strategies: only accept drinks you've watched being made, keep your drink with you at all times, don't accept drinks from people you've just met, and be wary of overly friendly strangers buying drinks. If you suspect your drink has been spiked: find trusted friends immediately, get to a safe location, seek medical attention, and report the incident to authorities. Safe socializing: buy your own drinks, go out with trusted companions, establish check-in systems with friends, and have plans for getting home safely.

78.

Keep Your Drink With You Always

Leaving drinks unattended gives others the opportunity to spike them with drugs or other substances. Never leave drinks: when going to the bathroom, while dancing or socializing, on tables when you step away, or with people you don't know well. Safe practices: take drinks with you when possible, ask trusted friends to watch drinks if you must leave them, finish drinks before leaving them unattended, and order fresh drinks rather than returning to abandoned ones. Drink monitoring techniques: use drink covers or coasters that show tampering, stay aware of your drink's taste and effects, watch for unusual bubbling or color changes, and trust your instincts if something seems wrong. Group safety: establish buddy systems with fellow travelers, take turns watching each other's drinks, create signals for when someone needs help, and intervene if you see someone's drink being tampered with. High-risk situations: crowded bars and clubs, festivals and outdoor events, situations where you don't know many people, and places with poor lighting or supervision.

79.

Know Your Alcohol Limits

Drinking too much makes you an easy target and puts you in dangerous situations you can't handle properly. Factors affecting alcohol tolerance: travel fatigue and dehydration, altitude changes, different alcohol strengths in different countries, medications you might be taking, and stress from travel. Signs you've reached your limit: difficulty making clear decisions, impaired coordination and balance, slurred speech or communication problems, memory gaps or confusion, and feeling overly emotional or aggressive. Safe consumption guidelines: drink one alcoholic beverage per hour maximum, alternate with water or non-alcoholic drinks, eat substantial food before and while drinking, and set a maximum number of drinks before you start. Risk assessment: understand that your normal limits may be lower while traveling, recognize that unfamiliar environments increase risks, be aware that local customs might encourage excessive drinking, and know that being alone increases vulnerability. Emergency planning: have contact information for reliable transportation, know the location of your accommodation, keep emergency cash separate from your main funds, and establish check-in times with people at home.

80.

Stay Hydrated with Water

Proper hydration is especially critical when traveling, as dehydration affects judgment and physical ability. Hydration needs increase: in hot or dry climates, at high altitudes, when walking long distances, when consuming alcohol, and when experiencing travel stress. Safe water sources: sealed bottled water from reputable brands, water treated with purification tablets, water that's been boiled for at least one minute, and water from reliable filtration systems. Avoid questionable water: tap water in areas with poor sanitation, ice made from untreated water, fountain drinks that may use local water, and water offered by strangers. Signs of dehydration: headaches and dizziness, fatigue beyond normal travel tiredness, dark yellow urine, dry mouth and lips, and confusion or irritability. Hydration strategies: carry water with you at all times, drink before you feel thirsty, monitor urine color as a hydration indicator, and increase intake in challenging conditions. Water safety equipment: portable water filters for extended travel, purification tablets for emergency situations, reusable water bottles to reduce waste, and electrolyte supplements for hot climates.

Clear thinking strategies: focus on facts rather than fears, prioritize immediate safety over other concerns, use your emergency plans and preparation, seek help from appropriate authorities and professionals, and take action rather than becoming paralyzed by fear. Emergency communication: learn basic emergency phrases in the local language, have key information written in the local language, understand how to communicate your location clearly, know your accommodation address and contact information, and have backup communication methods if your phone doesn't work. Testing and verification: verify that emergency numbers work from your phone, understand any special dialing requirements, know whether emergency services speak your language, research response times and procedures, and have backup plans if primary emergency numbers don't work.

Emergency communication: establish emergency communication protocols with your contacts, create simple code words for different types of emergencies, have backup communication methods if your phone fails, understand time zone differences for international contacts, and maintain regular contact schedules so people know when to worry if they don't hear from you.

Emergency spending priorities: transportation to safety as the top priority, communication costs for contacting help, basic food and accommodation needs, medical expenses that can't wait, and costs associated with replacing essential documents.
